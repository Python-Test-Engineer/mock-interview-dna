## Key Observation

## Business / Scientific Implication

The 11pp spread across neighbourhoods represents one of the largest effect sizes in the dataset, comparable to the age group effect. High-no-show neighbourhoods (Itararé, Jesus de Nazareth) should be prioritised for targeted interventions — community health workers, mobile clinics, or transport subsidies. The fact that high-volume Jardim Camburi has below-average no-shows suggests that proximity to well-resourced facilities correlates with better attendance. Conversely, the worst-performing neighbourhoods may lack adequate local health infrastructure, forcing residents to travel further — a hypothesis testable with geographic distance data.

## Deeper Analysis

The colour gradient reveals a continuous spectrum rather than discrete clusters, suggesting that neighbourhood no-show rates are driven by a combination of factors (income, transport access, clinic proximity, population age) rather than a single binary cause. The position of Centro (~20.5%, near overall average) is expected for a mixed commercial/residential area. The bottom performers (high no-show: Itararé at ~27%) are historically lower-income areas of Vitória, supporting the socioeconomic hypothesis. Importantly, this chart only shows the top 30 by volume — the remaining 51 neighbourhoods have smaller samples and may show even more extreme rates. The overall reference line at 20.2% falls in the middle-to-upper third of the distribution, meaning more than half of the top-30 neighbourhoods actually perform better than average.
