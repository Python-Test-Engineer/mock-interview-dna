## Key Observation

The Scholarship (Bolsa Família) no-show premium is remarkably consistent across all neighbourhood volume terciles: approximately 3-4 percentage points higher in every category. No Scholarship rates are stable at ~19-20% across all terciles, while Scholarship rates hold at ~23-24%. Crucially, neighbourhood volume does not moderate the welfare effect — the gap persists identically in low-volume peripheral areas (19.2% vs 23.1%), medium-volume (19.7% vs 23.0%), and high-volume central areas (19.8% vs 24.0%).

## Business / Scientific Implication

The consistency of the ~3.5pp Scholarship premium across all neighbourhood types rules out a purely geographic explanation. If the Scholarship effect were driven by "welfare recipients live in remote areas with poor transport," we would expect the gap to narrow in high-volume (typically more central, better-connected) neighbourhoods — but it does not. This suggests the barriers are socioeconomic and personal (childcare, lost wages from time off work, competing survival priorities) rather than geographic. Interventions for this group should focus on reducing the personal cost of attendance: flexible scheduling, extended hours, childcare facilities at clinics, or small financial incentives tied to attendance.

## Deeper Analysis

The flat blue line (~19-20% across terciles) is itself informative — it means that neighbourhood size/location has almost no effect on non-Scholarship patients' no-show behaviour. The slight uptick from 19.2% to 19.8% across terciles is within noise. The slight widening of the gap in high-volume neighbourhoods (4.2pp vs 3.3pp in medium vs 3.9pp in low) is marginal and may reflect the larger sample sizes in high-volume areas providing tighter estimates. The overall consistency across all three panels is strong evidence that the Scholarship effect is a genuine independent predictor, not an artefact of geography. At 9.8% prevalence, Scholarship recipients account for ~10,800 appointments; their ~3.5pp excess no-show rate translates to ~380 additional missed appointments attributable to welfare-related barriers.

## Confidence Assessment

**Confidence:** High
**Rationale:** The effect is consistent across three independent subgroups with large samples, ruling out confounding by neighbourhood volume. The 3-4pp gap is stable and reproducible.

## Suggested Next Steps

1. Test whether the Scholarship effect persists after controlling for age and lead time — welfare recipients may be younger or schedule differently
2. Pilot a targeted attendance support programme for Bolsa Família recipients in one high-volume neighbourhood: measure whether addressing practical barriers (transport vouchers, childcare, text-to-reschedule options) closes the 3.5pp gap
