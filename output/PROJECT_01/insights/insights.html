Medical Appointment No-Show Analysis — Vitória, Brazil
PROJECT_01 25 images analysed 6 scripts analysed 2026-03-18

Executive Summary

This analysis of ~110,000 medical appointments in Vitória, Brazil reveals that the 20.2% overall no-show rate masks two fundamentally different populations: same-day walk-ins (45% of volume, 5% no-show) and advance bookings (55% of volume, 29% no-show). Appointment lead time and prior patient no-show history are by far the strongest predictors — together they explain more variance than all demographic and clinical features combined. The SMS reminder system works (reducing no-shows by 6-8pp within lead-time strata) but appears counterproductive in raw data due to Simpson's paradox. Socioeconomic barriers (Scholarship/Bolsa Família recipients) drive a consistent 3.5pp no-show premium not explained by geography. The highest-risk micro-segment — hypertensive adolescents — reaches a 35% no-show rate.

Key Insights

01

Two Healthcare Systems in One: The Same-Day Divide

Source: 02_lead_days_distribution, 11_noshow_by_lead_days, 22_same_day_profile

Observation: 45% of all appointments are same-day with a 4.6% no-show rate, while the remaining 55% (advance bookings) have a 29% no-show rate. Same-day patients skew younger and healthier; advance bookings serve older chronic disease patients. The overall 20.2% rate is a misleading weighted average of two distinct systems.

Implication: All predictive models, overbooking algorithms, and intervention strategies should be built separately for same-day vs advance booking populations. Targeting same-day patients with reminders is wasteful; all resources should focus on the advance-booking cohort.

High — Based on ~50K same-day and ~60K advance appointments with unambiguous demographic differences.

02

Prior Behaviour is the Strongest Crystal Ball

Source: 17_repeat_patient_noshow, 24_patient_appointment_counts

Observation: Patients with 0% prior no-show history miss 17.5% of appointments; those with 51-75% prior history miss 39.7% — a 22.2pp spread, the widest of any single feature. Highly frequent visitors (10+ appointments) have the lowest no-show rate (16.5%).

Implication: A patient's prior no-show rate should be the centrepiece feature of any predictive model. A simple rule — "flag patients with >25% prior no-show rate for enhanced outreach" — would capture most at-risk patients with minimal model complexity.

High — Uses only chronologically prior data to avoid leakage; monotonic trend is robust.

03

The SMS Paradox: When Confounders Reverse the Truth

Source: 12_noshow_by_sms, 19_sms_paradox, 04_binary_flags_prevalence, 18_correlation_heatmap

Observation: Raw data shows SMS recipients have an 11pp higher no-show rate (27.6% vs 16.7%). When stratified by lead time, SMS actually reduces no-shows by 6-8pp within every stratum. The paradox arises because SMS was selectively sent to long-lead-time appointments (r=0.40). Only 32% of appointments received SMS.

Implication: SMS reminders should be expanded to all non-same-day appointments. The estimated 6pp within-stratum effect applied to ~60K advance bookings would recover ~3,600 appointments. Any ML model must handle the SMS/lead_days confound explicitly.

High — Within-stratum reversal is consistent across 3 lead-time buckets with large samples.

04

Age as a Lifecycle Predictor with a Dangerous Interaction

Source: 01_age_distribution, 10_noshow_by_age_group, 20_age_condition_heatmap

Observation: Adolescents/young adults (11-20: 25.2%, 21-30: 24.6%) are highest risk; elderly patients (61-70: 14.7%) are most reliable — a 10.5pp spread. The age × hypertension interaction reveals an extreme outlier: hypertensive adolescents hit 35.0%, the highest rate of any identifiable subgroup.

Implication: Age-specific interventions: digital nudges for 11-30, transport support for 81+, minimal intervention for the reliable 50-70 cohort. Hypertensive adolescents (35% no-show) demand targeted outreach as medically critical patients.

High for main age effect; Medium for hypertensive adolescent cell (small sample).

05

Socioeconomic Barriers Trump Geography

Source: 13_noshow_by_conditions, 23_scholarship_analysis, 15_noshow_by_neighbourhood, 21_neighbourhood_inequality

Observation: Bolsa Família recipients have a consistent ~3.5pp no-show premium that persists across low, medium, and high-volume neighbourhoods. Chronic condition patients (Hypertension, Diabetes) actually have lower no-show rates by 2-4pp. Neighbourhood rates vary by 11pp (Gini 0.138).

Implication: The Scholarship effect is socioeconomic, not geographic. Interventions should address personal barriers (lost wages, childcare, transport cost) rather than facility proximity. "Sicker patients attend better" — chronic conditions are protective.

High — Consistency across 3 independent neighbourhood terciles rules out geographic confounding.

06

Temporal Stability: A Persistent Structural Problem

Source: 08_appointments_over_time, 16_noshow_by_month, 25_weekly_noshow_timeseries, 07/14_weekday charts

Observation: The system operates at a stable capacity ceiling (~4,500/day). Monthly variation is only 2.3pp. Weekly fluctuations stay within ±1.5pp. Day-of-week effects are modest: Friday/Saturday worst (21-23%), Thursday best (19.3%).

Implication: The no-show problem is structural and persistent. A model trained on any month generalises well to others. Overbooking algorithms can use steady-state assumptions with minor day-of-week adjustments.

High for within-window stability; Medium for extrapolation beyond April–June.

07

Weak Linear Signals, Strong Non-Linear Effects

Source: 18_correlation_heatmap, 09_noshow_by_gender

Observation: Pearson correlations with noshow_flag are universally weak (max r=0.19 for lead_days). Age correlates at just r=-0.06 despite a 10.5pp spread. Gender is a complete non-predictor (0.3pp). Multicollinearity: Age↔Hypertension (r=0.50), lead_days↔SMS (r=0.40).

Implication: Tree-based models (XGBoost) strongly preferred over linear models. Feature engineering (prior_noshow_rate, same-day flag) provides more lift than raw demographic features. Multicollinearity clusters must be managed.

High — Mismatch between linear correlation and actual effect sizes confirms non-linearity.

Patterns Across Analyses

  1. Lead time is the thread through everything. It directly predicts no-shows, confounds the SMS effect, drives the same-day population split, and correlates with SMS receipt. It touches every other finding.
  2. Age and chronic conditions are tightly coupled. Age correlates with Hypertension (r=0.50) and Diabetes (r=0.43), both of which appear protective — but largely because they proxy for the reliable elderly cohort.
  3. Confounding is pervasive. SMS ↔ lead_time, age ↔ conditions, Scholarship ↔ age ↔ neighbourhood. Naive single-variable analysis produces misleading conclusions in at least 3 of 25 charts.
  4. Volume concentration creates leverage. Top 5 neighbourhoods hold ~25% of volume. A targeted intervention in just 5 neighbourhoods for advance-booking patients could reach ~15% of total volume with ~30% of total no-shows.
  5. Two hidden populations should never be merged. Same-day (young, acute, 5% no-show) vs advance (older, chronic, 29%). Every aggregate statistic is a misleading blend of these two groups.

Recommended Next Steps

High Priority

  1. Build segmented prediction model — Separate models for same-day vs advance bookings using prior_noshow_rate, lead_days, age, neighbourhood. Use XGBoost with AUROC.
  2. Expand SMS to all advance bookings — Currently 32% coverage; 6-8pp within-stratum effect justifies universal deployment. Est. recovery: ~3,600 appointments.
  3. Lead-time-proportional overbooking — Same-day: 0%. 1-7d: 5%. 8-30d: 15%. 31-90d: 20%. Match the empirical curve.

Medium Priority

  1. Tiered outreach for high-risk patients — >25% prior rate: phone calls. >50%: community health worker or same-day rebooking preference.
  2. Investigate Scholarship population — Cross-tab with age, lead time, and neighbourhood to design targeted support (transport vouchers, flexible scheduling).
  3. Audit neighbourhood data quality — Remove <50 appointment neighbourhoods from rate comparisons.

Exploratory

  1. Join external socioeconomic data — Census income, transit coverage, facility locations to predict neighbourhood-level rates.
  2. Test appointment-type effects — Specialty/provider data would likely be a top-5 predictor.
  3. Temporal expansion — 12+ months to decompose seasonal patterns and long-term trends.
